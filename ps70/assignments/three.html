Process

  • For this project, I was inspired by Valentines Day. I always get my partner flowers, and we play Minecraft together, so I thought I would give him a Minecraft flower! When you drop items in the game, they kind of go spinny spinny until you pick them up, so I thought it was a good opportunity to incorporate some circuitry into my gift to him.

    The flower (a Minecraft oxeye daisy) was laser cut and hand-painted (unfortunately, the lab didn't have white paint to finish it). The box was stolen from the depths of the fabrication lab. A yellow DC motor is glued to lid of the box, with a small hole on top to reveal the knob of a petentiometer and the axle of the motor. The pententiometer is wired to act as a variable resistor on the motor, controlling its rpm. And that's really it! It's very simple, since I'm very new to fabrication in general.

  • I'm embarrassed to say that this project idea didn't work out, but I thought it was worth documenting. My first idea was to cut a plane, attach it to a motor, and then have it go around in circles. I ended up getting a little lazy with the laser cutting, so I ended up not going with this idea. I swapped out the plane (design below) with a hand so that the kinetic sculpture would become (depending on your mood) a high-five machine or face-slapping machine.

    Anyways. The idea was that it'd be wired up in the same way as the minecraft flower project. The only difference was that, because I needed the hand to move in a circle, I needed to engineer a small key that would lock onto the axle of the motor. In the end, the hand was a little bit heavy, and making a container for the motor that needed such a wide berth ended up being too much work for the time I had this week. I was not as happy with my assignment for this week, even though my partner enjoyed it, so I'm hoping to do something more complex and fun next week.
